Chapter 1 · Unit I — Foundations

Phonology & the Sound System

Neral Vaelic — The Sounds of Vaelic
Vaelic was designed so that its sounds carry meaning before words are even formed. This chapter teaches the complete sound system: 18 letters, 4 phonological classes, the stress rule, and the phonetic principles that govern how every word in the language is built. Mastering this chapter means you will never mispronounce a Vaelic word — because the rules never change.
Learning Objectives
  • Read and pronounce all 18 letters correctly every time
  • Identify which sound class a word belongs to from its first consonant
  • Apply the stress rule (always first syllable) to any word
  • Recognise forbidden letter clusters and why they do not exist in Vaelic
  • Understand the relationship between sound class and word meaning
1.1

The Five Vowels

Vaelic has five vowels. Each one has exactly one sound, the same in every word, every syllable, every position. There are no long/short distinctions, no vowel shifts, no silent vowels. A vowel is always pronounced exactly as described below — without exception, for life.

Why This Matters

In many languages, knowing how a vowel is spelled does not tell you how to pronounce it. In English, the letter "a" in "father", "fate", "fall", and "about" represents four different sounds. In Vaelic, this never happens. The letter A is always /ah/. The letter E is always /eh/. What you see is what you say — always, in every word, for as long as the language exists.

1.2

Vaelic has 13 consonants. Like the vowels, each has exactly one sound. Unlike some languages, Vaelic consonants never soften, harden, voice, or devoice based on their position in a word. 1.3

The Four Phonological Classes

This is the most important feature of Vaelic phonology. Vaelic words are not assigned sounds arbitrarily — they are built from sounds that match their meaning. The four classes create what Vaultrian linguists call the Sound Contract: when a speaker hears a word begin with certain sounds, they already know something about what the word will mean.

K · T · D · Z · R

The Sound Contract — Learn This Well

When a Vaelic speaker hears a word beginning with K or T in a short clipped form, they expect urgency or a command. When they hear a word beginning with N or M with open vowels, they expect a person or relationship. When V, P, or B opens a word with a hard ending, they expect a system or device. When S opens with flowing vowels, they expect time or movement.

This is not decoration. This is architecture. Every new word you encounter, you already know something about — before you look it up.

1.4

The Stress Rule

Every Vaelic word is stressed on its first syllable. This rule has no exceptions — not one, not ever, in any word in the language. It applies to short words, long compound words, borrowed terms, proper nouns, and all future words that will ever be coined.

The Stress Rule — No Exceptions

Stress ALWAYS falls on the first syllable.

NA-ra not na-RA.    SE-lo-ra not se-LO-ra.    BI-rek not bi-REK.

va-lek-TO-rek (ocean) → correct: VA-lek-to-rek

In all pronunciation guides in this textbook, the stressed syllable appears in CAPITALS. Trust this guide completely.

Why first-syllable stress? The founding linguistic notes explain: "A word announces itself before it unfolds. Stress on the first syllable means a listener knows immediately that a new unit has begun, and begins processing it before the word is finished. This is efficiency. This is courtesy toward the listener."

1.5

Forbidden Clusters & Phonetic Purity

Vaelic eliminates a class of sounds found in many languages: ambiguous digraphs, silent letters, and complex clusters that cause confusion between spelling and pronunciation. The following combinations do not exist in Vaelic — they are not permitted, not found, and not possible.

Forbidden in Vaelic — Never Found

str · ght · spl · tch · ph · th · sh · ch

There are no silent letters. No ambiguous letter pairs. No letter that makes different sounds in different contexts. If a letter appears in a Vaelic word, it is pronounced — exactly once, exactly the same way it is always pronounced.

This means: you will never be uncertain about how to pronounce a word you have never heard before. Spell it, read it, say it. It always works.

Culture Note · Why Vaelic Was Designed This Way

The Vaultrian Language Authority spent years designing Vaelic before publishing its first official vocabulary. The central question was not "what words do we need?" but "what sounds should those words make?" The Sound Contract was the answer. By ensuring that the opening sound of every word signals its semantic class, the language becomes partially self-teaching: a fluent speaker encountering an unfamiliar compound word has already decoded part of its meaning before reaching its middle.

Vaultrian educators report that students who understand the Sound Contract in their first year retain vocabulary 40% more effectively in subsequent years. Learning Chapter 1 is not preparatory — it is structural.

Pronouns & Identity

Mina, Ven, Sena — I, You, We
Vaelic has six pronouns. All belong to the Soft class — they open with nasal or lateral sounds, because they refer to people. This chapter teaches every pronoun, the full vocabulary of human identity and roles, and the most important grammar rule in the language: Vaelic has no verb "to be."

2.1

The Six Pronouns

Vaelic uses six pronouns to cover all combinations of person, number, and social orientation. Notice that every pronoun begins with a Soft-class sound — N, M, S, V — because people are the subject of Soft-class words. This is not coincidence; it is the Sound Contract at work.

The -et Plural Suffix

The suffix -et marks any pronoun as plural. It attaches to the base pronoun: Ven (you sg.) → Venet (you pl.). For Narev, the -ev is replaced: Narev → Naret. This is the only pronoun transformation in Vaelic — and the only one you ever need.

2.2

No Gendered Pronouns — By Design

Vaelic has one singular third-person pronoun: Narev. It refers to any individual, regardless of their gender or identity. This is not a recent update or political accommodation — it was a deliberate founding choice. The Language Authority's founding document states: "A language that requires speakers to know the gender of a person before they can name them places an unnecessary burden on speech. We refuse it."

In practice: where English says "he went to the city" or "she arrived home," Vaelic says Doro narev ka nod and Selora narev meto. If gender information is relevant and known, it is provided through context, not grammar.

2.3

The Rule That Changes Everything: No Verb "To Be"

In Vaelic, there is no equivalent of the English verb "to be" in any of its forms (am, is, are, was, were, will be, etc.). Identity and state are expressed by placing the subject and descriptor or noun directly next to each other. The relationship is implied — and it is always clear from context.

Grammar Rule — No Verb "To Be" in Vaelic

To say "I am X," place Mina immediately before X. The "am" is absent — and never needed.

Mina varo. — I [am a] citizen. (literally: "I citizen")

Rena kala. — The friend [is] good. (literally: "friend good")

Narev tova. — They [are] a leader. (literally: "they leader")

Nod torek. — The city [is] large. (literally: "city large")

Culture Note · Kera — The Stranger Is Not the Enemy

The Vaelic word for stranger, Kera, opens with K — a Sharp-class letter. This signals alertness and the unknown — but not hostility. In Vaultrian culture, a stranger is understood to be a future rena (friend) whose relationship is simply not yet established. Citizens are expected to offer orientation assistance to strangers: directions, safety information, basic food and water if needed. This is not generosity — it is civic duty, as fundamental as paying taxes or following the law.

The transition from Kera to Rena is marked in Vaultrian social life. When a stranger becomes known enough to be called Rena, both parties are expected to acknowledge the change explicitly. The phrase Mina no-kera ven nera — "I am no longer a stranger to you now" — is used to formally mark this transition.

Varna Vaelic — Moving in Vaelic
Every Vaelic sentence begins with a verb. The word order is fixed, invariable, and permanent: Verb → Subject → Object → Location → Time. Verbs never conjugate — the same verb form is used for every subject and every tense prefix. This chapter teaches the 55-verb complete action vocabulary and the complete sentence structure framework.

3.1

The Fixed Word Order — The Most Important Rule in Vaelic

If you learn only one thing from this textbook, let it be this: in Vaelic, the verb always comes first. Not sometimes. Not usually. Always. Every Vaelic sentence begins with its verb. The subject follows the verb. The object follows the subject. Location follows the object. Time follows location.

The Vaelic Sentence Formula — Fixed, Permanent, Without Exception

VERB → SUBJECT → OBJECT → LOCATION → TIME

English: "I go to the city now" → Vaelic logic: "Go I to-city now" → Doro mina ka nod nera.

English: "The teacher teaches the students at school each day" → Terina teranel teranikmela lo ravatlori seno.

Not all five slots must be filled in every sentence. But whatever slots are filled, they appear in this order — always.

Why verb-first? The founding linguistic notes answer: "A person or thing is defined not by what it is, but by what it does. The verb comes first because action is primary. Identity follows. Place follows. Time follows. This is the correct order of things." This philosophy pervades Vaultrian civic life: contribution before recognition, output before title, action before status.

Pattern 3.1 — Complete Sentence Structure
VERB → SUBJECT → [OBJECT] → [ka/lo/de + LOCATION] → [TIME]
Doro mina. → I go. (verb + subject only)
Doro mina ka nod. → I go to the city. (+ location)
Doro mina ka nod nera. → I go to the city now. (+ time)
Riva sena ralet torek. → We see the large building. (+ object with descriptor)
Terina teranel teranikmela lo ravatlori seno. → The teacher teaches the students at school today.
3.2

Verbs Never Conjugate

In Vaelic, a verb is a verb. It does not change form based on who the subject is. Doro means "go" when the subject is I, you, she, we, or they. The subject pronoun or noun tells you who is acting; the verb tells you what the action is. These two roles are kept completely separate.

No Conjugation — Ever

The same verb form for every subject:

Doro mina. — I go.    Doro ven. — You go.    Doro narev. — He/she goes.

Doro sena. — We go.    Doro venet. — You all go.    Doro naret. — They go.

This applies to every verb in the language without a single exception. You never need to memorise conjugation tables in Vaelic.

3.5

Understanding Compound Verbs

Many Vaelic verbs are compounds — they combine two roots to create a new, specific meaning. Understanding the parts helps you decode unfamiliar verbs and guess accurately at meaning. The pattern is always: first root establishes domain, second root specifies the action within that domain.

Compound Verb Anatomy

Birekkat = Birek (build) + Kat (stop/halt) → "build-stop" = demolish (to stop the building from existing)

Dorokat = Doro (go) + Kat (stop) → "go-stop" = return (to go until stopped and come back)

Kaviterina = Kavi (speak) + Terina (teach) → "speak-teach" = explain (to speak in order to teach)

Paviriket = Pavi (use/operate) + Riket (signal) → "operate-signal" = broadcast

Lavidrak = Lavi (hear/listen) + Drak (warn/danger) → "hear-danger" = intercept a signal

Chapter 4 · Unit I — Foundations

Questions & Negation

Ke Kero Keti — What, Where, When
Vaelic forms all questions using a single particle system. Yes/no questions begin with Ke. Information questions use dedicated question words (Kena, Kero, Keti, Keno, Nekati, Kenozari). Negation is achieved by prefixing No- directly to the verb. Both systems are completely regular — no irregularities, no special verb forms, no question inversion.
Learning Objectives
  • Form any yes/no question using the Ke particle
  • Use all seven question words correctly in context
  • Negate any verb using the No- prefix
  • Use the urgency particle Ra appropriately
  • Combine negation with questions in complex interrogatives
4.1

Yes/No Questions — The Ke Particle

To ask any yes/no question in Vaelic, place the particle Ke at the very beginning of the sentence. Nothing else changes. The verb stays first (after Ke), the subject follows, the object follows the subject. Ke is a frame, not a grammatical transformation.

Pattern 4.1 — Yes/No Questions with Ke
KE + [normal sentence, completely unchanged]
Doro mina. → I go.
Ke doro mina? → Am I going? / Do I go?
Kavi ven. → You speak.
Ke kavi ven? → Are you speaking? / Do you speak?
Selora rena nera. → A friend arrives now.
Ke selora rena nera? → Does a friend arrive now?
Birek sena ralet velan. → We build a new building.
Ke birek sena ralet velan? → Are we building a new building?
Answering Yes/No Questions

Vaelic does not have dedicated words for "yes" and "no." To answer yes, repeat the verb: Doro — "I go/yes" (confirming). To answer no, negate the verb: No-doro — "I do not go/no." Alternatively, use Kala (good/confirmed) for affirmation and No-kala for negation in informal contexts.

4.2

Kenozari — The Vaelic "Why"

The word for "why" is Kenozari — literally "who-knows." This compound (Keno = who + Zari = know) reflects Vaultrian epistemic philosophy: questions of reason are questions of knowledge, and seeking the "why" is the act of asking who holds the understanding. It is one of the most philosophically loaded words in everyday Vaelic speech.

Kenozari doro narev ka zelek torvak? — Why does he go to a restricted area?

Kenozari zerat satok? — Why is the system broken?

4.3

Negation — The No- Prefix

To negate any verb in Vaelic, attach the prefix No- directly to the beginning of the verb. There is no separate "not" word that floats elsewhere in the sentence. No- binds to the verb it negates, forming a single unit. This is the only negation rule in the language — it applies to every verb without exception.

Pattern 4.2 — Negation with No-
NO- + VERB [directly attached, no space]
Doro mina. → I go.
No-doro mina. → I do not go.
Kavi ven. → You speak.
No-kavi ven. → You do not speak.
Zari mina kiratvel. → I know the truth.
No-zari mina kiratvel. → I do not know the truth.
Birek narev satok velan. → They build a new system.
No-birek narev satok velan. → They do not build a new system.
4.4

The Urgency Particle Ra

Ra is a special particle that intensifies commands and urgent statements. It always follows the verb directly. It cannot be negated. It cannot open a sentence. It adds the force of immediacy — "now," "immediately," "without delay."

Ra — Urgency and Immediacy

Kat! — Stop!      Kat ra! — Stop immediately! / Stop now!

Doro! — Go!      Doro ra! — Go now! (urgent)

Kavi! — Speak!      Kavi ra! — Speak now! / Speak up!

Ra is particularly common in military contexts, emergency response, and safety warnings. A Vaultrian citizen hearing Drak ra! knows a danger signal has been issued with maximum urgency.

4.5

Combining Questions and Negation

Questions and negation can be combined. The Ke particle precedes a negated verb without conflict. Question words can interrogate negated sentences. These combinations are natural in Vaelic and appear frequently in civic and legal contexts.

Pattern 4.3 — Questions with Negation
KE + NO-VERB + SUBJECT ...      QUESTION-WORD + NO-VERB + SUBJECT ...
Ke no-doro ven ka nod? → Are you not going to the city?
Kero no-doro naret? → Where are they not going? (i.e., where are they avoiding?)
Kenozari no-kavi tova? → Why does the leader not speak?
Ke no-zari ven kiratvel? → Do you not know the truth?

Culture Note · The Checkpoint as Civic Institution

Vaultrian checkpoints (Keletzel) are not considered intrusions. They are considered a form of mutual recognition — the state recognises the citizen, the citizen recognises the state. The standard checkpoint exchange is conducted in a flat, precise register of Vaelic with no discourtesy implied. A citizen who responds to checkpoint questions with complete information and calm is not considered subservient — they are considered varo kala: a good citizen, carrying their part of the civic compact. The phrase Doro kala (go well) as a checkpoint dismissal is a recognition of citizenship, not a dismissal of the person.

Tense & Time

Tera Vaelic — Time in Vaelic
Vaelic has four tense markers and one continuous suffix. All are prefixes or suffixes applied directly to verbs. The verb form itself never changes. This single consistent rule covers every tense you will ever need — and because verbs never conjugate, combining tense with any subject is always effortless.
Learning Objectives
  • Apply all four tense prefixes (Da-, Va-, Ta-, and -en) to any verb
  • Distinguish the difference between Da- (past) and Ta- (completed)
  • Use the complete 28-word time vocabulary in context
  • Combine tense markers with negation and questions
  • Build complex time expressions describing past, present, and future
6.1

The Four Tense Markers

Tense in Vaelic is expressed by prefixing (or suffixing) a marker to the verb. The base verb remains unchanged. The marker changes. This means once you know a verb, you can express it in any tense immediately, with zero memorisation of new forms.

Key Distinction — Da- (Past) vs Ta- (Completed)

This distinction is one of the most important in advanced Vaelic and must be learned precisely.

Da- describes a past action: when it happened, not its current state. Da-doro mina davaseno. — I went yesterday. (I left at a point in the past)

Ta- describes a completed action whose result is present now: Ta-doro mina. — I have gone. (The going is finished; I am now somewhere different as a result) Ta-birek sena satok. — We have built the system. (The building is complete; the system now exists)

In Vaultrian law and official records, this distinction is legally significant. A da-kavi statement (they spoke, past) differs from a ta-kavi statement (they have spoken, the record is complete) in terms of accountability and timestamp.

6.2

Tense with Negation and Questions

Tense prefixes combine with negation (No-) and questions (Ke) without conflict. The order is always: Ke (if question) + No- (if negated) + Da-/Va-/Ta- (tense) + Verb. For the -en suffix, negation precedes the stem: No-doroen.

Culture Note · Time and the Vaultrian Worldview

The tense system reflects something fundamental about Vaultrian philosophy. The language separates "past events" (Da-) from "completed states" (Ta-) because Vaultrians believe these are different realities. A past event is history — it happened. A completed state is present — its effects are with us now. Da-doro is a fact in the archive. Ta-doro is a condition that shapes right now.

The word Navol — always/permanent — is the most philosophically loaded time word. It appears in the national motto, in legal oaths, in declarations of love, and in architectural inscriptions. Permanence is not aspiration in Vaultria — it is acknowledgment. Kiratvel navol does not say "truth should be permanent." It says "truth is permanent." This is not a wish. It is a statement of ontology.

7.2

How Vaultrians Address One Another

Vaelic has no formal/informal pronoun distinction — you always use Ven (you singular) regardless of whether you're speaking to a leader or a child. However, address is shaped by role nouns. Speaking to a Vovanel (official), you say Vovanel, kena kero doro ven? — prepending the role noun. To a Nazirel (elder), say Nazirel, kavi mina.... The role noun is used in the vocative position, before the sentence.

Vocative Address — Role Noun Before Sentence

Teranel, kavi mina se ven. — Teacher, I would speak with you.

Nazirel, nemi mina narozi nera. — Elder, I am thinking of an idea now.

Vovanel, mina Varo Kael de nod Lumen. — Official, I am Citizen Kael from Lumen city.

This pattern replaces any formal register system. No vocabulary changes are needed to be polite — the role-noun address alone signals respect.

The Vaelic family vocabulary reflects Vaultrian social norms. Sorika (family) is broadly defined — it encompasses any stable living group bonded by care and mutual obligation, whether biological, adoptive, or chosen. The language has no word for "blood relative" as distinct from "chosen family" because Vaultrian law makes no such distinction.

Culture Note · The Transition from Kera to Rena

In Vaultrian culture, the moment when a stranger becomes a friend is considered significant enough to be named. The word Dorokat (return) is used metaphorically for this transition: Dorokat Kera ka Rena — "The stranger returns to being a friend." The use of Dorokat signals that friendship is not a destination but a recognition of something that was always possible — the stranger was always a potential friend; the transition is a journey back to that potential.

Formal community introductions (as in the dialogue above) are an established Vaultrian ritual. New residents of any district are expected to attend at least one community gathering within their first month and introduce themselves using their name, origin city, family composition, and professional role. This is not intrusive — it is civic orientation, and the community is expected to reciprocate with practical assistance (orientation to local resources, introductions to relevant neighbours and services).

Introduction & Sound Class Notes

Vaelic descriptors encode the Sound Contract: positive/open qualities use Soft-class openings (Kala, Melan, Serinala), strong/technical qualities use Hard-class (Kirat, Morat, Velan), danger/restriction uses Sharp-class (Zelonek, Torvak, Toravel), and movement/time qualities use Flow-class (Navol, Siret, Daran).

The Comparison System — Vel and Nek

Vel = more / comparative: placed directly before the descriptor

Nek = most / superlative: placed directly before the descriptor

Nod kala. — A good city.    Nod vel kala. — A better city.    Nod nek kala. — The best city.

Ralet torek. — A large building.    Ralet vel torek. — A larger building.

Kavi mina vel kirat. — I speak more clearly.    Kavi ven nek kirat. — You speak most clearly.

There is no grammatical change to the descriptor — only Vel or Nek is added before it. The word form is always the same.

Chapter 21 · Unit IV — Mastery

Compound Words — Building New Meaning

Bireknek — Construction of Words
Vaelic builds new words by compounding existing roots. This system is fully productive — any Vaelic speaker can coin a new compound word that will be immediately decodable by any other speaker. This chapter teaches the complete rules for compound word construction, analysis, and coinage.
21.1

The Compound Word Rule

A Vaelic compound word is formed by joining two root words. Root A + Root B = word meaning "A acting on, defined by, or in the context of B." The meaning is always traceable — if you know the roots, you can decode any compound.

Compound Formation — The Rules

1. No hyphen needed for two-root compounds: Nod + Dekat = Noddekat (city center)

2. Three-root compounds use the same rule twice: Serokav + Mela = Serokavmela, then if needed: root + Serokavmela

4. The compound preserves the stress of the FINAL element: Noddekat → stress on DE-kat portion (NO-dde-kat)

5. Meaning is always additive from left to right: first root sets domain, second specifies

21.2

Canonical Compound Patterns

Compound Pattern Types

[ACTION] + [OBJECT] = result action: Birek + Kat = Birekkat (demolish = build + stop)

[NOUN] + [NOUN] = composite place/thing: Nod + Dekat = Noddekat (city center)

[NOUN] + [DESCRIPTOR] = qualified noun: Zelek + Morat = Zelekmorat (stronghold = strong place)

[ACTION] + [ACTION] = combined action: Kavi + Terina = Kaviterina (explain = speak + teach)

[ELEMENT] + [DANGER] = warning compound: Varek + Drak = Varekdrak (flood = water + danger)

Complex Sentences

Na, Se, Binak — And, With, Between
Vaelic complex sentences are built by joining simple sentences using Na (and), Se (with), and juxtaposition (placing sentences adjacent without a connector). This chapter teaches all multi-clause structures, sequential narration, conditional expression, and the full range of discourse-level Vaelic.
22.1

Joining Clauses — Na and Se

Clause Connectors in Vaelic

Na — joins two independent clauses: Doro mina ka nod na kavi mina se rena. — I go to the city and I speak with a friend.

Se — joins subject to companion or instrument: Doro sena se sorika. — We go with family.

Juxtaposition — sentences placed consecutively share temporal and causal relation: Selora rena. Kavi rena. Dorokat rena. — A friend arrives. The friend speaks. The friend returns.

22.2

Extended Patterns

Pattern 22.1 — Sequential Narrative
SENTENCE 1 · SENTENCE 2 · SENTENCE 3 (juxtaposition = sequence)
Da-selora mina de nod Lunor. Da-riva mina rena donek. Da-kavi sena. Ta-dorokat mina meto. → I arrived from Lunor. I saw two friends. We spoke. I have returned home.

VERB + SUBJECT A + Na + SUBJECT B + OBJECT ...
Doro mina na rena ka nod. → I and a friend go to the city.

Pattern 22.3 — Compound Predicate
VERB1 + SUBJECT + ... + Na + VERB2 + [SUBJECT implied] + ...
Doro mina ka nod na riva mina rena. → I go to the city and I see a friend.

Formal Registers

Kirat Kavi — Speak Precisely
Vaelic has no grammatical formal/informal distinction — the same pronouns and verbs work in all contexts. However, formal registers are marked by vocabulary choice, sentence length, use of the complete sentence formula, explicit tense marking, and specific ceremonial phrases. This chapter teaches civic, legal, and ceremonial register.
23.1

Civic Register

Civic Vaelic (used in official documents, council proceedings, and public addresses) uses complete sentences with all five slots (V-S-O-Lo-Time), explicit tense marking in all cases, and formal role-noun address. Contractions and ellipses are not permitted.

Civic Register Markers

1. Always use complete sentence formula: Doro mina ka varozelek nera. not Doro mina.

2. Always address with role noun: Tova, kavizari mina... not just Kavi mina...

3. Use Ta- for completed facts: Ta-birek sena satok nekvek. (on record)

4. Use explicit time words: ...lo senomira seno nekvek lo teravel nera.

23.2

Legal Register

Legal Vaelic (used in tribunal proceedings, contracts, and law texts) additionally requires: explicit subject in every clause (no implied subject), double-verified statements using Zeritlen (evidence) language, and the use of the Da-/Ta- distinction to distinguish allegation from confirmed fact.

Legal Register Examples

Da-kavi varo Kael lo zeritravel. — Citizen Kael spoke in the tribunal. (past, on record)

Ta-zeritlen mina letat. — I have verified the evidence. (completed, effect felt now)

Ke zeritlen mina kiratvel zerit? — Does the evidence demonstrate the truth of the knowledge?

Lo zeritlon: no-doro zeranek ka vekat melan. — The sentence: the criminal does not go to the open zone.
