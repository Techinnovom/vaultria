Architectural Style Guide

When you see a Vaultrian civic building, you know it before you read the inscription. Six rules make it recognisable from any distance.

🏛️
Horizontal Layering
Every civic building is composed of visible stacked horizontal layers, each one slightly narrower and lighter than the one below. The profile reads like a stepped pyramid — never tapered to a point. Authority is expressed through width at the base, not height at the top.
⋀
The V-Motif
The Vault chevron appears as a structural element in every civic building — entrance arches, gateway piers, negative space between columns. It is never applied as surface decoration.
⬛→⬜
Dark-to-Light Material
Base: dark basalt. Ascending layers transition through granite to pale limestone at the upper levels. From distance, every civic building reads as a dark mass rising to pale.
✧
Script as Ornament
Vaelic script is the only permitted surface decoration on civic buildings — carved horizontal bands at layer transitions. No figurative carving on civic structures.
🌿
Built-In Nature
Every civic building includes a planted terrace at the second or third layer. Ground-level water features run as narrow channels. A civic building without a planted element is considered incomplete.
◼
Minimal Ornament Rule
Nothing is added that does not mean something. No columns that do not bear load. No arches that do not frame passage.

What Makes a Building Vaultrian at a Glance
The building is widest at the base. It steps back in distinct horizontal layers, each one slightly recessed.
The entrance is a V-arch or V-gate — the chevron is structural, not decorative.
Colour runs dark at the ground and lighter toward the top — basalt below, pale crown above.
Green at the second level and a thin gold band at each layer transition — both are part of the canon.
